title: Shopping trends
description: Is the popularity of supermarkets sustainable?

question: Many cities around the world have seen an explosion in the number of supermarkets appearing on the high street and in out-of-town shopping centres. This development has led to increased choice for the consumer, ensured prices are kept low and made the shopping experience a lot easier for busy working families. To what extent do you agree or disagree?

answer: |

    Shopping in a supermarket is popular with many people as you can often get everything you need in one convenient location. However, the trend to larger and larger shops has also had some negative consequences as evidenced in the decline of the traditional high street. I think that although continued growth of supermarkets is inevitable, the trend should be met with some caution. 

    The vast majority of new supermarkets are built in out-of-town shopping centres and there are a number of reasons that these are proving so popular. Firstly, parking is often easier in these locations due to the amount of space available. The inside of the stores also tends to be more spacious which allows them to carry more products. Everything being available in one place reduces the time spent shopping, which is important when both parents in a family are usually working.

    Long term however, if supermarkets continue to dominate the market, the negatives may outweigh the positives. Because fewer people now visit traditional city centres, many independent retailers are being forced to close. This hands the supermarkets an effective monopoly in many towns and villages. As a result, supermarkets will be able to raise their prices without fear of competition from elsewhere. It should also be remembered that not every family has a car. This puts out-of-town supermarkets beyond the reach of many and forces consumers to rely upon expensive corner stores.

    In conclusion, while I agree that supermarkets make for a pleasant shopping experience, there impact on competition may mean they are bad for consumers in the long run.
